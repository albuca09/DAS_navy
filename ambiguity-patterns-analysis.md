## Technical analysis of ambiguity patterns

The results indicate that samples classified as ambiguous are not randomly distributed throughout the dataset, but instead concentrate under specific geometric and spatial conditions. In the evaluated set, comprising **1033 samples**, the estimated ambiguity rate was **13.26%**, based on the empirical thresholds `cross_abs = 552.55`, `err_xyz = 652.02`, and `cross_ratio = 3.47`. These values indicate that the operational definition of ambiguity captured a relatively small yet structurally well-characterized subset of high-error cases.

A first relevant finding is that ambiguity is strongly associated with the **geometric offset** between the true shot location and the surface geometry used as the local reference. The variables showing both the highest importance in the auxiliary classifier and the strongest statistical separation between ambiguous and non-ambiguous cases were `geom_surface_nearest_dist_xy_m`, `geom_surface_nearest_dist_3d_m`, `geom_surface_topk_mean_dist_3d_m`, and `geom_surface_topk_median_dist_3d_m`. In all these variables, the ambiguous cases exhibited a marked increase in distance. The minimum horizontal distance to the surface, represented by `geom_surface_nearest_dist_xy_m`, increased from **183.25 m** in non-ambiguous cases to **364.81 m** in ambiguous cases, with `cohen_d = 1.710`, indicating a very large effect size. Similarly, the minimum 3D distance (`geom_surface_nearest_dist_3d_m`) increased from **183.44 m** to **365.13 m** (`cohen_d = 1.711`), while the mean of the local nearest 3D distances (`geom_surface_topk_mean_dist_3d_m`) increased from **191.06 m** to **373.84 m** (`cohen_d = 1.713`). From a physical standpoint, these results suggest that increasing the separation between the target and the local fiber geometry reduces the spatial discriminability of the inverse problem and favors solutions with a strong transverse error component.

Beyond distance, the results reveal a pronounced spatial component, indicating that ambiguity concentrates in **specific fiber regions**. The variables `geom_surface_nearest_ch`, `local_nearest_surf_idx`, `local_nearest_surf_ch`, `dir_center_channel_used`, and `geom_surface_topk_ch_mean` appear simultaneously among the most important and among those with the largest effect sizes. For instance, `geom_surface_nearest_ch` showed a mean of **2297.91** for non-ambiguous cases and **5051.23** for ambiguous cases, with `cohen_d = 1.641`. A similar behavior was observed for `local_nearest_surf_idx`, which increased from **2267.93** to **5021.23** (`cohen_d = 1.641`). This pattern indicates that the phenomenon is not solely due to a globally more difficult localization task, but rather to **particular fiber segments** in which ambiguous errors occur much more frequently.

The regional analysis reinforces this interpretation. The bins `6900` and `6600`, each containing **9 samples**, exhibited an ambiguity rate of **100%**, with mean 3D errors of **1273.57 m** and **1259.47 m**, respectively. In both cases, the transverse error component was substantially larger than the longitudinal one: in bin `6900`, `mean_abs_cross = 1185.96 m` and `mean_abs_along = 463.99 m`; in bin `6600`, `mean_abs_cross = 1171.14 m` and `mean_abs_along = 463.11 m`. Bin `6100`, comprising **53 samples**, showed an ambiguity rate of **67.92%**, with `mean_err_xyz = 729.67 m`, `mean_abs_cross = 650.78 m`, and `mean_abs_along = 317.37 m`. These values characterize a regime clearly dominated by transverse error. In contrast, bins such as `2400`, `1800`, and `0`, although still presenting non-negligible errors, exhibited zero ambiguity rate, suggesting that **high error alone is not sufficient**; the local geometric structure of the error is decisive.

Another important aspect concerns the **local geometric support**. The variables `geom_surface_n_near_points`, `geom_total_block_length_m`, and `geom_surface_total_block_length_m` showed strong mean reductions in ambiguous cases. The number of nearby surface points (`geom_surface_n_near_points`) dropped from **998.47** to **107.52** (`cohen_d = -1.185`). The total geometric block length (`geom_total_block_length_m`) decreased from **1045.17 m** to **107.62 m** (`cohen_d = -1.225`), while the total surface block length (`geom_surface_total_block_length_m`) decreased from **1003.59 m** to **107.62 m** (`cohen_d = -1.180`). These findings suggest that ambiguous cases tend to occur under conditions where the local geometric representation is poorer, less continuous, or less extensive, thereby reducing the robustness of the association between the observation and the true position.

The **local fiber curvature** also appears to contribute to ambiguity, although with less pronounced effect than distance and channel location. The variable `local_curvature_xy` appears among the most important features in the classifier, and the most ambiguous regions frequently exhibit high curvature and directional change values. For example, bin `6900` presented `mean_curvature = 2.1455` and `mean_turn_angle = 2.1852 rad`, whereas bin `6600` presented `mean_curvature = 2.0371` and `mean_turn_angle = 2.0557 rad`. These values are substantially higher than those observed in less problematic regions, such as bin `0`, where `mean_curvature = 0.0719` and `mean_turn_angle = 0.0698 rad`. This suggests that geometries with sharper directional changes promote spurious transverse projections, thereby increasing the difficulty of correctly discriminating the shot location.

There is also evidence of a relevant vertical component, although secondary relative to the transverse error in the horizontal plane. The variable `geom_surface_nearest_dist_z_m` increased from **5.06 m** to **13.64 m** between non-ambiguous and ambiguous cases (`cohen_d = 1.231`), while `geom_bh_nearest_dist_z_m` increased from **11.63 m** to **26.44 m** (`cohen_d = 1.162`). In addition, some ambiguous regions exhibited high `mean_abs_z`, such as bin `5600`, with **35.25 m**. These findings suggest that vertical separation between the shot and the reference geometry also contributes to localization degradation, although the main mechanism associated with ambiguity remains the **dominance of the transverse component**.

Taken together, the results point to a consistent mechanism: ambiguity arises preferentially in specific fiber segments where **greater offset relative to the surface geometry**, **reduced local geometric support**, and, in several cases, **higher curvature or directional change** coexist. Under these conditions, the error ceases to be predominantly longitudinal and becomes dominated by the transverse component, characterizing a regime of **local geometric ambiguity**. Therefore, the observed phenomenon does not correspond to a mere indiscriminate increase in error, but rather to a structured failure mode strongly dependent on the local geometry of the problem.

A concise formulation suitable for inclusion in a manuscript is the following:

> The analysis of samples classified as ambiguous revealed that ambiguity is strongly associated with unfavorable local geometric conditions. Compared with non-ambiguous cases, ambiguous samples presented approximately twice the horizontal and tridimensional distance to the nearest surface geometry (`183.25 m` vs. `364.81 m` and `183.44 m` vs. `365.13 m`, respectively), with large effect sizes (`cohen_d ≈ 1.71`). Moreover, these samples concentrated in specific fiber segments, particularly at higher channel indices (`~5051` vs. `~2298`), indicating a strong spatial dependence of the phenomenon. Regional analysis identified bins with ambiguity rates as high as `100%`, in which the transverse error component largely exceeded the longitudinal one, characterizing a geometrically oriented error regime. Overall, the results suggest that ambiguity arises from the combination of geometric offset, reduced local support, and increased geometric complexity of the fiber, especially in segments with higher curvature and directional change.

Finally, an important methodological caveat should be emphasized. The auxiliary classifier reached `AUC = 1.0`; however, this value **should not be interpreted as evidence of generalizable predictive performance**, since the model was both trained and evaluated on the same dataset. Therefore, at this stage, the classifier should be understood as an **exploratory tool**, intended to rank variables and highlight patterns associated with ambiguity rather than to demonstrate out-of-sample predictive capability. In a scientific manuscript, it is advisable to state explicitly that feature importances and descriptive statistics were used as tools for interpreting the phenomenon, while predictive validation itself should be addressed in future studies using strict group-, region-, or trajectory-based data partitioning.
